Music is not only about the sounds you hear; it's equally about the silence between the notes. Speed reading has a similar secret. It's not just about the words you see, but about the gaps your brain fills in automatically. When you read fast, your mind predicts the next word, the next idea, and even the next sentence before your eyes get there.

Last month, I watched a jazz pianist perform live. His fingers moved like water, but what caught my attention was his pauses. He would stop for a split second, and in that silence, the audience leaned forward. We all completed the melody in our heads. That's exactly how speed reading works. You don't need to see every letter. Your brain composes the rest.

To practice this, try reading a paragraph while covering the last two words of each line. Guess what they are. Most of the time, you'll be right. This is because language has patterns. Once you recognize those patterns, you can jump from one anchor word to another. For example, in the sentence "The sun set behind the mountains, painting the sky orange," your eyes can land on "sun," "mountains," and "orange" — and your brain will paint the whole picture.

Start with five minutes of daily practice. Use articles, short stories, or even song lyrics. Read them in chunks, not in letters. Then slowly increase your speed by using a metronome or a simple timer. Read one line per tick, then two lines, then three. But always stop after each paragraph and ask: what was the core idea? If you can answer in five words, you're winning.

This text has about 380 words. Try to finish it in 80 seconds. Then close your eyes and replay the jazz pianist in your mind. Remember: speed is not the goal. Understanding is. And understanding often lives in the empty spaces — between notes, between lines, between words.
