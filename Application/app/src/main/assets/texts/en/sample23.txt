Title: Why We Yawn

Everyone yawns, but no one fully understands why. For years, scientists thought yawning was about oxygen — that we yawn to take in more air when we're tired. But studies have shown that yawning doesn't actually increase oxygen levels. A newer theory suggests that yawning is a brain-cooling mechanism. When your brain gets slightly too warm, a yawn brings in cooler air through the sinuses, lowering brain temperature and improving alertness.

What's even stranger is that yawning is contagious. Seeing someone yawn triggers your own yawn, and even reading about yawning can make you yawn (are you yawning right now?). This contagiousness is linked to empathy. People who are more empathetic tend to catch yawns more easily. It's a social signal — a way of syncing up with others, showing that you're part of the group.

Speed reading has a connection to yawning too. When you read for long periods, your brain can overheat from concentration. The more you focus, the warmer your brain gets, and the more you may yawn. It's your body's way of resetting, cooling down so you can continue. If you find yourself yawning while reading, don't fight it. Take it as a sign: your brain is due for a short reset.

Here's a practical tip: when you feel a yawn coming while speed reading, embrace it. Then take 30 seconds to look away from the page, blink, and breathe deeply. That quick reset will cool your brain and restore focus. Sometimes the fastest way to speed up is to pause for a breath — a lesson we can learn from the simple yawn.
