Title: The Focus of Archery

Archery is a sport of stillness. The archer stands motionless, draws the bow, and releases with precision. Success depends not on strength, but on focus. The slightest distraction—a flicker of movement, a wandering thought—can send the arrow off course. This is the same focus that speed reading demands. When your attention is scattered, your eyes jump erratically and comprehension drops.

In archery, the archer does not aim at the target. They aim at a single point within the target—the center of the center. Similarly, speed readers don't aim at the whole page. They aim at key sentences, main ideas, and critical transitions. Everything else is secondary. This precision allows them to move through text with speed and accuracy.

A technique borrowed from archery: before you start reading, choose one question you want to answer from the text. That question becomes your "target." Every time your focus wanders, gently pull it back to that question. This keeps your attention anchored, just as the archer's eyes stay fixed on the center. Your reading speed will increase because your brain has a clear purpose.

The next time you read, think like an archer. Stand still. Breathe. Choose your point of focus. Then release your attention into the text. You'll find that when your mind is calm and your goal is clear, the words seem to fly toward you—just as the arrow flies toward the target.
