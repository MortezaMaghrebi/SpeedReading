Title: The Doorway of Attention

Attention is the doorway through which all reading enters. Without it, even the simplest text becomes impossible. With it, complex ideas flow effortlessly. Speed reading isn't about moving your eyes faster — it's about holding your attention steady while your eyes move. When attention wavers, speed drops. When attention locks in, speed soars.

The problem is that attention has a rhythm. It peaks and valleys naturally. You can't force it to stay high; you have to work with it. That's why the best speed readers take short breaks every 15-20 minutes. They don't fight fatigue. They respect it. They step away, breathe, and return. This keeps attention fresh and speed consistent.

A practical method: use the "Pomodoro" style. Read intensely for 10 minutes, then take 2 minutes to close your eyes and mentally summarize what you just read. This dual action — reading and recalling — trains your brain to hold attention longer. Over time, you'll find you can maintain high focus for 30, 40, even 60 minutes without losing speed.

Think of attention as a muscle. The more you exercise it, the stronger it gets. But even the strongest muscle needs rest. So the next time you feel your reading speed slipping, don't blame your eyes. Check your attention. Is it still in the room, or has it wandered? Bring it back gently, and your speed will follow.
