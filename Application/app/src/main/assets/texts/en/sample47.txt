Title: The Curiosity of Children

Children ask an average of 300 questions a day. "Why is the sky blue?" "Where does rain come from?" "How do birds fly?" Their curiosity is relentless and fearless. They don't worry about sounding silly—they just want to know. This natural curiosity is what makes children such fast learners. They absorb information like sponges because they approach everything with wonder and openness.

Speed reading adults can learn from children. When you read with curiosity, your brain processes information faster. Questions activate your mind, turning reading from a passive activity into an active search. Instead of just receiving words, you're hunting for answers. This engagement increases focus, comprehension, and naturally, speed. The more curious you are, the faster you read.

A simple technique: before reading, become a child again. Ask questions about the text before you even open it. "What will I learn here?" "Why should I care?" "What surprises me about this topic?" Write your questions down. Then read to find the answers. Your brain will stay engaged because it has a mission. Children don't need to force focus—they're naturally focused because they're naturally curious.

The next time you read, channel your inner child. Ask "why" and "how" and "what if." Let your curiosity lead the way. You'll find that when you're genuinely interested, speed takes care of itself. Children know this secret intuitively. Now it's time for adults to rediscover it.
