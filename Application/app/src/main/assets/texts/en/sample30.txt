Title: The Octopus Mind

The octopus is one of the most intelligent creatures on Earth, yet its brain is organized completely differently from ours. Two-thirds of its neurons are distributed throughout its arms, meaning each arm can think and act independently. This decentralized intelligence allows the octopus to solve puzzles, open jars, and escape from tanks — all while its central brain processes other information. It's a living example of distributed processing.

Speed reading works similarly. You don't need your brain to process every word individually. Instead, you can distribute the work: your peripheral vision catches upcoming words while your central focus handles the current ones. Your brain's pattern-recognition system fills the gaps. The more you practice, the more natural this distributed processing becomes, allowing you to read faster without losing comprehension.

Here's a technique inspired by the octopus: train your peripheral vision. Try reading with your eyes fixed on the center of a line, but notice how you can still see the beginning and end of the line. Practice expanding this awareness. Eventually, you'll be able to read entire phrases at a glance without moving your eyes word by word. This is the octopus approach to reading — many "arms" working together.

The next time you read, think like an octopus. Don't rely on a single point of focus. Spread your attention across the page. Trust your brain's ability to process information from multiple angles. Your mind, like the octopus, is more capable than you give it credit for. Use that distributed intelligence, and your reading speed will surprise you.
