Title: The Language of Whales

Whales don't have written language, but they have songs that travel for miles across the ocean. These songs change over time, spreading from one population to another like cultural trends among humans. Scientists have discovered that whale songs have grammar, rhythm, and even regional dialects. This shows that intelligence isn't about having hands or building cities — it's about communication and adaptation.

What makes whale songs remarkable is their efficiency. In a single phrase, a whale can convey information about food location, danger, or mating availability. Speed reading works similarly. Your brain doesn't need every detail to understand a message; it needs the key signals. Just as whales listen for specific patterns in the ocean, speed readers look for keywords, transitions, and main ideas.

Here's a technique inspired by whales: before reading a text, listen for the "song." Skim the first and last sentence of each paragraph. Notice repeated words and phrases. This gives you the melody of the text without reading every note. When you go back for a full read, your brain already knows the tune and can follow it quickly.

The next time you read, think like a whale. Don't get trapped in every wave; listen for the song that carries the meaning. Language is more than words — it's pattern, rhythm, and intention. The faster you recognize the pattern, the faster you'll understand the message.
