Title: The Precision of Isaac Newton

Isaac Newton was obsessed with precision. He didn't just observe the apple fall—he calculated its acceleration. He didn't just wonder about the moon—he measured its orbit with mathematical exactness. His laws of motion and universal gravitation are among the most precise scientific principles ever discovered. Newton believed that understanding the world required careful observation and exact measurement.

Speed reading requires a similar kind of precision. Not precision in reading every word, but precision in knowing what to read and what to skip. The most efficient readers don't read everything—they read with intention. They know exactly where to focus and where to glance. This precise selection allows them to move through text quickly while capturing the essential meaning.

A technique from Newton: before reading, define your target clearly. What specific information are you looking for? Newton didn't study everything—he studied what mattered. Similarly, you don't need to read every article or book from cover to cover. Scan the table of contents, headings, and summaries. Find your gravitational center—the core idea that pulls everything else together.

The next time you read, think like Newton. Be precise about your purpose. Know what you're looking for. Don't waste energy on irrelevant details. Your reading speed will increase because you're not guessing—you're measuring, selecting, and focusing with the precision of a scientist.
