Title: The Library of Alexandria

In ancient Egypt, there was a library that held more knowledge than any place on earth. Scholars traveled from across the world to study there. They came to read scrolls on astronomy, medicine, philosophy, and poetry. But the library wasn't famous because it had the most books—it was famous because of how people read there. Scholars didn't just collect scrolls. They read them with purpose. Each scholar would enter the library with a question, not with an empty mind. They would scan hundreds of scrolls in a day, not reading every word, but searching for answers to their questions.

One scholar, a young man from Greece, was overwhelmed by the number of scrolls. He tried to read everything. He would start at the beginning of a scroll and read every single line, determined not to miss anything. After a month, he had only finished five scrolls. He felt discouraged. He went to the head librarian and said, "There are too many scrolls. I will never finish." The librarian smiled and said, "You are not supposed to finish. You are supposed to find what you need."

The young man didn't understand at first. He thought reading meant reading everything. But the librarian showed him how other scholars worked. They would read the first and last sentence of each section. They would skim for key names and numbers. They would take notes on patterns they noticed. They didn't read to finish; they read to find. They trusted their brains to notice what was important. The young man tried this method. Within a week, he had read fifty scrolls and found answers to questions he had been carrying for months. He realized that the library's power wasn't in its shelves—it was in the minds of those who knew how to search, not just how to read.
