Title: The Patience of Albert Einstein

Einstein is known as a genius, but he called himself a "patient man." He didn't solve the theory of relativity overnight. He thought about it for years, turning the same questions over in his mind, refusing to rush. He once said, "It's not that I'm so smart, it's just that I stay with problems longer." That's the secret many speed readers miss: patience creates speed.

When you rush through a text without understanding, you waste time. When you slow down for the complex parts and stay with them, you actually finish faster overall. Patience is not the opposite of speed — it's a tool for speed. Einstein's thought experiments took time, but once he had the solution, everything clicked into place instantly. Speed reading works the same way: understanding one difficult paragraph well saves you from reading it five times.

Here's a technique: when you hit a confusing sentence, don't skip it. Don't panic. Just stop for three seconds. Re-read it once with patience. Let your brain sit with the words. Often, clarity arrives in that moment of stillness. This is called "productive pause," and it's one of the most powerful tools in speed reading. It's better to pause for three seconds than to re-read the whole page.

The next time you feel the urge to rush, remember Einstein. Genius wasn't about moving fast — it was about staying long enough to see what others missed. Speed reading isn't about cutting corners; it's about knowing where to spend time and where to sail through. Patience, paradoxically, is the fastest route to understanding.
