Title: The Art of Doing Nothing

We live in a world that worships busyness. But doing nothing is not laziness — it's a skill. When you sit still without a phone, without a book, without any goal, your brain enters a state called the "default mode network." This is where creativity, memory consolidation, and problem-solving actually happen. Speed reading benefits from this too, because a rested brain reads faster than a tired one.

Here's a paradox: the more you force yourself to read quickly, the slower you often become. Tension slows your eyes. Relaxation speeds them up. So before you start a speed reading session, spend two minutes doing absolutely nothing. Stare at a wall. Watch your breath. Let your mind wander. This resets your focus and prepares your brain for rapid word recognition.

Try this experiment. Read a page at your normal speed and note the time. Then take a three-minute break where you do nothing — no music, no scrolling, no thinking about your to-do list. Just sit. Then read another page and time yourself again. Most people find their speed increases by 15-20% after the break. That's because doing nothing is actually doing something for your brain.

So remember: speed doesn't come from pushing harder. It comes from stepping back. The next time you feel stuck or slow while reading, put the text down. Close your eyes. Do nothing for one minute. When you open your eyes again, your brain will be ready to fly through the pages. Sometimes, the fastest way forward is to stop moving entirely.
