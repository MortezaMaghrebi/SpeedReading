Title: The Hidden Rivers Underground

Beneath our feet, there's a world we rarely see. Underground rivers flow through caves and cracks, carving tunnels over millions of years. These hidden waterways supply fresh water to cities, farms, and forests — often without us even knowing they exist. Scientists estimate that more than 30% of the world's freshwater lies underground, moving silently through rock and soil.

Reading works the same way. Beneath the surface of every text, there's a hidden current of meaning. The main idea is rarely stated in every sentence; it flows beneath, connecting paragraphs like water through stone. Speed reading is about sensing that underground river. You don't need to read every word to understand the flow; you just need to feel the current.

A useful exercise: when you finish a paragraph, pause for five seconds and ask yourself, "What's the underground idea here?" Don't look for the exact words; look for the unseen connection. This trains your brain to look beneath the surface, making your reading faster because you're following the current, not just the words.

The next time you read, remember the hidden rivers. They carry more water than the visible ones. And just like those underground streams, the most important ideas in any text are often the ones that aren't spelled out — they're the ones you feel flowing beneath the sentences.
