Title: The Calligrapher Who Wrote Too Fast

A calligrapher named Li was famous for his speed. He could finish a page of beautiful writing faster than anyone. But one day, a customer returned his work and said, "Your letters are rushed. I can't read them." Li was embarrassed. He tried to slow down, but his hand kept moving fast out of habit. He decided to try something different: he wrote the same page three times. The first time, he wrote as fast as he could. The second time, he wrote at a medium pace. The third time, he wrote slowly, focusing on each stroke.

When he compared the three pages, he noticed something. The fastest page was unreadable. The medium page was readable but uneven. The slow page was perfect—but it took too long. Then he realized the problem wasn't speed. It was rhythm. On the medium page, some strokes were fast and some were slow. That inconsistency made it look rushed. He practiced until his medium speed became steady, not fast. His work improved, and he could write faster than before—but now every letter was clear.

Li learned that true speed comes from control, not from rushing. When his strokes were even, his writing looked faster than it actually was. Reading is the same: when you read at a steady, comfortable pace, you actually finish faster than when you rush and have to re-read.
