Title: The Curiosity of Leonardo da Vinci

Leonardo da Vinci was not just a painter—he was an inventor, anatomist, engineer, and scientist. His notebooks are filled with sketches of flying machines, human dissections, and hydraulic systems. What drove him was not fame or money, but an insatiable curiosity. He wanted to know how everything worked: the human heart, the flow of water, the flight of birds. He once said, "Study without desire spoils the memory."

Speed reading benefits from the same kind of curiosity. When you're genuinely interested in a topic, your brain processes information faster. Your eyes move quicker because your mind is already engaged. Curiosity eliminates the need to force concentration. It comes naturally. The more curious you are, the less effort you need to read quickly.

Here's a technique inspired by da Vinci: before reading a text, write down three questions you want to answer. They can be simple or complex. "Why did the author write this?" "What's the main argument?" "How does this connect to what I already know?" These questions activate your curiosity. Your reading becomes a hunt for answers, which naturally accelerates your speed.

The next time you read, approach the text like da Vinci approached the world. Be endlessly curious. Ask questions. Look for connections. Speed reading is not about rushing—it's about being so engaged that you forget you're reading at all. That's the da Vinci way: curiosity so deep that speed becomes effortless.
