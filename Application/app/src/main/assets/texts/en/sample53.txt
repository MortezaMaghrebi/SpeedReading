Title: The Story of Thomas Edison and the Lightbulb

Thomas Edison didn't invent the lightbulb on his first try. He didn't invent it on his hundredth try, either. He tried thousands of materials for the filament—cotton, wood, bamboo, even human hair. Each time, the filament would burn out after a few seconds or minutes. Each failure was a dead end. But Edison didn't throw away the whole project after each failure. He just crossed that material off his list and moved to the next one.

What did Edison do differently? He didn't treat each failure as a stop sign. He treated it as information. He learned something from every burned filament. He noticed which materials lasted longer, which glowed brighter, which were cheaper. He didn't try to fix the failed filament—he just moved forward and tried something new. He didn't reread the same paragraph of his notes over and over. He kept experimenting, kept moving.

Now think about your reading. When you hit a sentence you don't understand, what do you do? Do you stop and stare at it? Do you reread it five times? Edison could have done the same with his failed filaments—he could have stared at them, hoping they would suddenly work. But he didn't. He moved forward. He knew that sometimes, the answer isn't in the place where you're stuck. The answer might be in the next sentence, the next paragraph, the next page.

Edison didn't understand how to make a working lightbulb until he had tried thousands of ways that didn't work. He didn't find the answer by staying in one place. He found it by moving forward, trying new things, and letting the failures inform his next steps. Reading is the same. If you're stuck on a sentence, don't stay there. Move forward. Sometimes the meaning of a difficult sentence becomes clear only after you've read the next few sentences. The lightbulb doesn't light up while you're staring at it—it lights up when you keep moving.
