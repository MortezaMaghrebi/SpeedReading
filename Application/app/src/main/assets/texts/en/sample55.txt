Title: The Girl Who Asked Why

There was a girl named Lila who asked "why" more than anyone in her village. Her parents would tell her to eat, and she would ask, "Why do we need food?" Her teacher would say the earth is round, and she would ask, "Why isn't it flat?" The other children laughed at her. But Lila didn't stop. She wasn't trying to be difficult. She was trying to understand. For her, a fact without a reason felt like a door without a handle—you could see it, but you couldn't open it.

One day, Lila found a book in the library that had no pictures. She opened it, and every page was filled with long, heavy paragraphs. The other children put it back. But Lila stayed. She didn't understand the first sentence, but she didn't close the book. She said, "Why can't I understand this?" And instead of giving up, she read the next sentence. Then the next. Then the next. She didn't try to memorize every word. She just kept reading, letting her questions lead her forward.

What did Lila do differently from the other children? She didn't read to finish. She read to find answers to her questions. The words she didn't understand didn't stop her—they made her more curious. She would think, "I don't know this word yet," and she would keep going. She found that the text often explained itself later. A word she didn't understand on page one would become clear on page three. A sentence that felt confusing would make sense after she read the next paragraph. She learned that understanding doesn't always come immediately—it often comes a few steps later.

Lila didn't become the smartest person in her village because she understood everything on the first try. She became smart because she didn't stop when she didn't understand. She kept going, kept asking, kept reading. And slowly, the heavy paragraphs became lighter. The long sentences became clearer. She realized that difficult texts are not walls—they are doors. And every door opens when you keep moving forward, even when you can't see what's on the other side.
