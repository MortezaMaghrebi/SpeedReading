Title: The Story of J.K. Rowling

Before J.K. Rowling became one of the wealthiest authors in history, she was a struggling single mother living on welfare. She wrote the first Harry Potter book in cafés while her baby slept beside her. Publishers rejected her manuscript twelve times before one finally said yes. Rowling didn't give up. She kept writing because she believed in her story. Today, her books have sold over 500 million copies worldwide.

What can Rowling's story teach us about speed reading? Resilience. The first time you try speed reading, you may feel slow and frustrated. You might lose focus or feel like you're not understanding anything. That's normal. That's your "twelfth rejection." But every time you try, you get a little better. Each attempt builds momentum. Rowling didn't succeed overnight—she succeeded because she refused to stop.

A technique from Rowling's process: break your reading into small, manageable pieces. She didn't write the entire Harry Potter series at once—she wrote one chapter at a time. Speed reading works the same way. Don't aim to read a whole book in one session. Start with one page. Then another. Then another. Small wins lead to big achievements.

The next time you feel discouraged by your reading speed, remember Rowling. She faced failure after failure, but she kept going. Every page you read is a small victory. Every session is progress. The story of your reading success is still being written—and like Rowling's, it will have a happy ending if you refuse to give up.
