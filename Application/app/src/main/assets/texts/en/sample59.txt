Title: The Runner Who Couldn't Breathe

There was a runner named Amir who trained for years to win a marathon. He ran every day, pushing himself harder each time. But he had a problem: in the middle of his runs, his breathing would become shallow and fast. His chest would tighten, and he would have to stop. He tried running slower. He tried running faster to get it over with. Nothing worked. His coach watched him struggle. She noticed that Amir wasn't paying attention to his breath. He was so focused on the finish line that he forgot to breathe with rhythm.

The coach gave him a simple instruction: "For the first mile, count your steps and match your breath to them. Inhale for four steps, exhale for four steps. Do not think about the finish line. Only think about your breath." Amir tried it. For the first few minutes, it felt strange. He wanted to stop counting and start pushing. But he forced himself to stay with the rhythm. Slowly, his chest relaxed. His legs felt lighter. He wasn't going faster, but he was going smoother.

After a week, Amir noticed something. When he lost his breath rhythm, his mind also lost focus. He would start thinking about the pain, the distance, the time, and his breathing would become shallow again. But when he returned his attention to his breath—just four steps in, four steps out—his mind would clear. He realized that his breath was like an anchor. Every time he brought his attention back to it, everything else became easier. He didn't need to push harder. He just needed to stay present.

On race day, Amir didn't start fast. He started with his breath. When other runners passed him, he didn't speed up. He stayed with his rhythm. In the last mile, the runners who had sprinted ahead slowed down, gasping. Amir was steady. He kept his rhythm and passed them one by one. He didn't win because he was the strongest runner—he won because he was the most present runner. He learned that focus is not about staring harder at the goal. It's about maintaining your rhythm when everything around you tries to break it. And in reading, focus is the same: not about forcing your eyes to move faster, but about keeping your attention steady while the words flow past.
