Title: The Persistence of Thomas Edison

Thomas Edison failed thousands of times before inventing the light bulb. When a reporter asked him about it, he famously said, "I have not failed. I've just found 10,000 ways that won't work." This persistence is what made him one of the greatest inventors in history. He didn't see mistakes as failures—he saw them as steps toward success.

Speed reading requires the same mindset. You won't master it overnight. You'll lose focus, skip words, and feel frustrated. But each mistake is feedback, not failure. With each attempt, your brain learns what works and what doesn't. Just like Edison's filament experiments, every "wrong" try brings you closer to the right rhythm.

Here's an exercise: when you lose your place or read a paragraph without understanding, don't get frustrated. Smile. Tell yourself, "I just found one way that doesn't work." Take a breath. Then continue. This mental shift reduces anxiety, which is one of the biggest blockers of reading speed. Relaxation, not force, is what unlocks faster reading.

The next time you struggle, remember Edison. He didn't become a genius because he got everything right. He became a genius because he got everything wrong—until he got it right. Speed reading is the same journey. Every stumble is a step forward. Persist, and the speed will come.
