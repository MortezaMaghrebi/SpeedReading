Title: The Memory of Elephants

Elephants are known for their extraordinary memory. They can remember water sources they visited decades ago, recognize individual humans they haven't seen for years, and even recall the calls of specific family members from across the savanna. Their brains are larger than any other land animal, with a highly developed hippocampus—the region responsible for memory and learning.

What makes elephant memory remarkable is its efficiency. They don't remember everything; they remember what matters. Water, danger, family, food. This selective memory allows them to survive in harsh environments. Speed reading works the same way. You don't need to remember every word—you need to remember what matters. The key ideas, the structure, the conclusion. Everything else is just detail.

A practical exercise: after reading a page, take 10 seconds to recall just three things. Not more. This forces your brain to identify the most important information. Over time, this selective recall becomes automatic. You'll read faster because you're not trying to memorize everything—you're just capturing the essentials, like an elephant finding water in a dry season.

The next time you read, think like an elephant. Don't try to hold everything in your memory. Trust that your brain will retain what's important. Let go of the rest. Speed reading is not about perfect recall—it's about smart recall. And nothing is smarter than an elephant's memory.
