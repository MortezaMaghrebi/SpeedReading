Title: The Power of Walking

Walking is the most underrated form of exercise. It requires no equipment, no gym membership, and no special skills. Yet, studies show that regular walking improves cardiovascular health, reduces stress, and boosts creativity. A simple 30-minute walk can increase blood flow to the brain, improving focus and memory. It's one of the best things you can do for both your body and your mind.

What does walking have to do with speed reading? Everything. When you walk, your brain enters a state called "relaxed alertness." This is the same state that allows you to read faster and comprehend better. Tension and stress slow your eyes. Relaxation speeds them up. Walking before a reading session can calm your nervous system and prepare your brain for rapid word processing.

Here's a practical tip: if you're stuck on a difficult passage, close the book and go for a short walk. Let your subconscious process the text while your body moves. Often, clarity comes when you're not forcing it. The rhythm of walking mirrors the rhythm of reading—steady, flowing, and natural. Your brain loves this rhythm.

The next time you sit down to read, don't start with the book. Start with your feet. Take a five-minute walk to clear your mind and oxygenate your brain. When you open the page, you'll find that your eyes move faster, your understanding is deeper, and the words seem to flow effortlessly. Walking is not a distraction from reading—it's preparation for it.
