Title: The Story of Colonel Sanders

Colonel Harland Sanders didn't become a success until he was 65 years old. Before that, he was a failed farmer, a failed soldier, a failed salesman, and a failed businessman. He was fired from multiple jobs. He had little money. But he had one thing: a secret recipe for fried chicken. Sanders traveled across the United States, knocking on doors, offering his recipe to restaurants. He was rejected 1,009 times before he found someone to say yes.

What does Sanders' story teach us about speed reading? Persistence. The first time you try speed reading, you may feel like a failure. You might reread sentences, lose focus, or feel like you're not making progress. That's okay. That's your "first thousand rejections." But if you keep practicing, if you keep reading, your speed will improve. Sanders didn't succeed because he was lucky—he succeeded because he refused to give up.

A technique from Sanders: treat every reading session as a "visit" to a new restaurant. Some sessions will be productive; others won't. Don't judge yourself based on one session. Keep showing up. Keep reading. Consistency is more important than intensity. Sanders knocked on doors every day, not just once. You should read every day, even if only for a few minutes.

The next time you feel discouraged by your reading speed, remember Colonel Sanders. He was rejected over a thousand times, but he never stopped. Every page you read is a "knock on the door." Some will open; some won't. But eventually, with persistence, the door to speed will open. And when it does, you'll be reading faster than you ever imagined.
