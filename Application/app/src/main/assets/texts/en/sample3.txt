We spend our whole lives walking forward, but have you ever tried walking backwards? It forces you to trust what you can't see, to rely on memory and peripheral senses. Speed reading is like walking backwards through a text. You don't catch every detail in order, but you trust your brain to assemble the whole picture from fragments.

Last year, I joined a group of elderly people who walked backwards every morning in the park. They said it improved their balance and memory. One of them, an 82-year-old retired teacher, told me: "When I read fast, I do the same thing. I don't go from A to Z. I jump from A to M to Z, and my mind fills the rest." That changed everything for me.

To practice this style of reading, try this technique: read only the first sentence of each paragraph, then skip to the last sentence. Then go back and read the middle. Most of the time, you'll understand the core message without reading every line. This is because writers usually put their main idea at the beginning and their conclusion at the end. The middle is just support.

Another exercise: take a page and turn it upside down. Read it backwards, from the bottom right to the top left. It sounds crazy, but it forces your brain to recognize words as whole shapes, not as letters in a sequence. After two minutes of this, when you turn the page right-side up, your reading speed will have increased by at least 20%. I've tested this on my students, and it works every time.

But remember, the goal is never to skip meaning. The goal is to find meaning faster. So after every quick read, pause for ten seconds and summarize. Say it out loud or write it down. If you can't summarize, you've gone too fast. Slow down a little and try again.

This text contains about 370 words. Aim to read it in 85 seconds. Then close your eyes and imagine walking backwards through a garden. What did you see? What did you miss? That's exactly the art of speed reading — moving forward while feeling backward, and still arriving at understanding.
