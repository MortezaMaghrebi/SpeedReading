Title: The Carpenter Who Built a Chair Company

In a small village, there was a carpenter named Hiro. He made beautiful chairs, one at a time. Each chair took him three days. He carved every curve by hand, sanded every surface until it was smooth, and polished every joint until it was perfect. His chairs were the best in the village. But he could only make two chairs a week. People loved his work, but they had to wait months to get one.

Hiro wanted to share his chairs with more people. He decided to hire workers. He taught them how to carve, sand, and polish. But the first batch of chairs they made was not like his. Some had rough edges. Some had uneven legs. Some were not strong enough. Hiro was disappointed. He wanted to go back to making chairs alone. But then he watched his workers more carefully. He noticed something: they were trying to copy his movements exactly. They were moving their hands the way he moved his hands, but they didn't understand why he moved them that way.

Hiro stopped training. Instead, he started explaining. He told them what to look for in the wood, not just what to do with their hands. He showed them how to test the balance of a chair before finishing it. He taught them the principles—not just the steps. Soon, the workers began to understand. Their chairs improved. They still made mistakes, but they learned from them. Hiro didn't check every chair anymore. He trusted his workers.

Now, Hiro could make fifty chairs a week. Not because he worked faster, but because he had built a system. His company became famous. People still said his chairs were the best—but now everyone could buy one. Hiro realized that true success wasn't about making chairs alone. It was about building something that could grow without him. And he learned that teaching principles, not just methods, is what allows others to carry your work forward.
