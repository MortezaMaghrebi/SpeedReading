Title: The Mirror of Words

Every text you read is not just information — it's a reflection of your own mind. When you read, you're not receiving ideas; you're constructing them based on your experiences, beliefs, and memories. Speed reading works better when you understand this: you don't need to absorb every word; you just need to activate the right parts of your memory.

Think of reading as a conversation. Each sentence is a question, and your brain provides the answer from its own storehouse. The faster you read, the more you rely on prediction. Your brain guesses what comes next, compares it with what's actually there, and adjusts. This prediction cycle is what makes speed possible. The more knowledge you have, the better your predictions, and the faster you read.

A practical technique: before reading any text, spend 30 seconds brainstorming everything you already know about the topic. Write down three keywords. This "pre-ignites" your brain's neural pathways. When you start reading, you'll recognize familiar concepts instantly and navigate new ones more easily. Your brain isn't starting from zero; it's connecting new information to existing networks.

The next time you pick up a book or article, remember: you're not a blank page. You're a mirror. And the faster you recognize what you already know, the faster you can absorb what you don't. Speed reading is ultimately about speeding up recognition, not about speeding up learning.
