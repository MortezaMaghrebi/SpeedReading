Title: Why the Sky Is Blue

The sky appears blue because of a phenomenon called Rayleigh scattering. Sunlight contains all colors of the spectrum. When it passes through Earth's atmosphere, the shorter blue wavelengths scatter more easily than the longer red wavelengths. This scattered blue light reaches our eyes from all directions, making the sky look blue. At sunset, the light travels through more atmosphere, scattering the blue out and leaving the reds and oranges.

This scientific fact is a perfect metaphor for speed reading. Just as the atmosphere filters sunlight, your brain filters the information you read. You don't need to absorb every word—only the ones that matter. The shorter, less important details scatter away, while the key ideas—the "reds and oranges"—remain clear and visible. Speed reading is about learning to see what stays, not what scatters.

Here's a practical tip: when reading a dense text, focus on the core concepts first. Don't get lost in the "scattering" details. Ask yourself: what is the main point? What is the author trying to prove? Once you have that, the details become easier to place. You'll read faster because you're not trying to process everything at once—you're letting the unnecessary details scatter naturally.

The next time you look up at a blue sky, remember: it's blue because of filtering. Your brain does the same thing when you read fast. It filters, it scatters, it focuses. Trust this process. Let the unimportant details scatter away, and you'll find that what remains—the core meaning—is clearer than ever.
