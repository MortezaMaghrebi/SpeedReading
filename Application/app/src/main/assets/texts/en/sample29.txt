Title: The Coral Reefs

Coral reefs are often called the "rainforests of the sea." Though they cover less than 1% of the ocean floor, they support nearly 25% of all marine life. These vibrant underwater cities are built by tiny animals called polyps, which secrete calcium carbonate to form hard skeletons. Over thousands of years, these skeletons accumulate into massive structures visible from space.

What makes coral reefs remarkable is their efficiency. In a chaotic ecosystem, they create order. Fish, turtles, sharks, and countless other species find food, shelter, and breeding grounds there. Speed reading works similarly: it creates order from the chaos of words. By focusing on key ideas and patterns, you can navigate through dense text without getting lost. The structure is already there — you just have to find it.

A useful technique: when reading a complex text, imagine you're a fish swimming through a reef. Don't stop at every detail. Move with the current, noticing the large formations and avoiding the sharp edges. In reading, those formations are main ideas; the edges are confusing details you can revisit later. Let your eyes flow like water through the structure of the text.

The next time you face a difficult page, remember the coral reefs. They thrive by working with their environment, not against it. Speed reading is the same: work with the text, not against it. Find its structure, follow its flow, and you'll move through it faster than you ever thought possible.
