Title: The Power of Deep Breathing

Most people breathe shallowly, using only a fraction of their lung capacity. This shallow breathing keeps the body in a low-grade stress state, triggering the sympathetic nervous system — the "fight or flight" response. But deep, diaphragmatic breathing does the opposite. It activates the parasympathetic nervous system, which calms the heart, lowers blood pressure, and signals the brain that it's safe to relax.

Here's the connection to speed reading: when you're relaxed, your eyes move more smoothly. Tension in your neck and shoulders restricts blood flow to the eyes, making them tired and slower. A few deep breaths before reading can release that tension, improving eye movement and focus. Many professional speed readers use breathing exercises as a warm-up before their sessions.

A simple technique: before opening a book, sit up straight. Exhale completely. Then inhale slowly through your nose for four seconds, hold for four seconds, and exhale for six seconds. Repeat three times. This is called "box breathing," and it's used by athletes, military personnel, and even surgeons to steady their focus. It primes your brain for high performance.

The next time you sit down to read, don't start with your eyes. Start with your breath. Take two minutes to breathe deeply and deliberately. You'll find that when your body is calm, your reading speed naturally increases. After all, reading isn't just an act of the eyes — it's an act of the whole nervous system. Breathe deeply, and read faster.
