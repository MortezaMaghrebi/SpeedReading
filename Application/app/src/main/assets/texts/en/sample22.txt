Title: The Curiosity of Marie Curie

Marie Curie didn't become famous because she was lucky. She became famous because she asked questions that no one else was asking. While other scientists studied visible phenomena, she was drawn to the invisible — radiation. She spent years working in a cold, damp shed, processing tons of pitchblende to isolate a tiny amount of radium. Her curiosity was stronger than discomfort, stronger than fatigue.

What made Curie extraordinary wasn't just her discoveries; it was her method. She read voraciously, but she didn't read everything with the same intensity. She skimmed to find what was relevant, then dove deep when something caught her attention. This is exactly what speed reading teaches: know when to skim and when to sink. Not all text deserves the same effort.

Curie's greatest lesson for readers is this: curiosity accelerates comprehension. When you truly want to know something, your brain processes information faster. Your eyes move quicker because your mind is already racing ahead, predicting, connecting, understanding. Interest is the ultimate speed booster — far more powerful than any technique.

The next time you read, channel your inner Curie. Ask questions before you start. What puzzles me about this topic? What do I want to uncover? Then let your curiosity guide your eyes. You'll find that speed comes naturally when you're hunting for answers, not just passing time.
