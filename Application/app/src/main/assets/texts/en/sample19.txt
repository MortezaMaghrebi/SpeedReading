Title: The Architecture of Words

Every text is a building. The title is the front door. The first paragraph is the entrance hall. Each paragraph is a room. And the conclusion is the roof that ties everything together. When you speed read, you're not examining every brick; you're walking through the house to understand its layout. You trust that the architect (the writer) designed it with purpose.

The problem is that many readers get lost in the rooms. They study the wallpaper, count the tiles, and inspect the furniture. By the time they reach the roof, they've forgotten the entrance. Speed reading is about seeing the floor plan first. You notice the corridors, the main rooms, and the exits. Later, if you need details, you can always go back.

Here's a practical method: before reading, spend 30 seconds on the title, headings, and first sentence of each paragraph. This is your "blueprint." Now when you read fully, your brain already knows which rooms are important and which are just decoration. This awareness alone can increase your speed by 30-40%.

The next time you open a book or article, think like an architect. Don't get lost in the details. Understand the structure first. Speed reading isn't about skipping; it's about knowing where to slow down and where to move fast. Every building has load-bearing walls and decorative arches. Know the difference, and you'll read with purpose and speed.
