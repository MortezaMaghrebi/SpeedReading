Title: The Rhythm of Reading

Reading has a rhythm, just like music. When you read slowly, it's like a sad ballad — each word dragged out. When you read fast, it's like jazz — syncopated, energetic, alive. Speed reading isn't about eliminating the rhythm; it's about changing the tempo. Your eyes can learn to dance across the page, hitting the beats of meaning rather than the syllables of sound.

The secret is to read in pulses. Instead of a continuous line of words, see each sentence as a series of beats: a noun, a verb, an object. Your eyes move from beat to beat, not word to word. This is how musicians read sheet music — they don't look at every note; they see the phrase, the chord, the progression. Speed readers do the same with language.

A practical exercise: take any sentence and identify its core three words. Read only those. Then expand to five words. Then seven. Each time, your eyes are jumping, not sliding. Practice this for five minutes a day, and your reading speed will naturally become more rhythmic and less mechanical.

Think of your eyes as a jazz drummer. They don't hit every fraction of a second. They land on the accents and let the silence carry the rest. The next time you read, feel the page. Not just the words, but the space between them. That space is where rhythm lives. And rhythm, not pressure, is the real engine of speed.
