Title: The Compass of Curiosity

Curiosity is the most underrated tool for speed reading. When you're genuinely interested in a topic, your brain processes words faster without any extra effort. It's not magic — it's biology. Curiosity releases dopamine, which heightens focus and accelerates pattern recognition. The more curious you are, the faster you read, automatically.

Here's the trick: before you start any text, ask yourself three questions. What do I want to learn? What surprises me about this topic? What would I ask the author? These questions activate your brain's reward system. Suddenly, reading isn't a task — it's a treasure hunt. Your eyes move faster because your mind is racing ahead, searching for answers.

Try this experiment. Take two short articles — one on a topic you love, one on a topic you find boring. Time yourself on both. You'll likely read the interesting one 30-50% faster with better comprehension. That's not a coincidence. Interest bypasses the need for techniques. When you care, speed follows naturally.

So the next time you have to read something you think is dull, don't force yourself to focus. Force yourself to get curious. Find one thing you don't know. One question the text might answer. Turn reading into a scavenger hunt. Curiosity is the fastest path to speed because it removes resistance. When you're excited, your brain practically races ahead on its own.
