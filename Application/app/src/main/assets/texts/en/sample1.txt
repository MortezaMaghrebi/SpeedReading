Title: The Art of Seeing Faster  

Speed reading is not about rushing. It's about training your brain to recognize patterns instead of decoding every single letter. Think of it like looking at a forest, not just trees. When you read slowly, you see words one by one. When you read fast, you see phrases, ideas, and whole sentences in a single glance.  

Last week, I tried an experiment. I took a novel with 300 pages and decided to read it in one evening. At first, it felt impossible. My eyes were tired, and my mind kept wandering. But then I used a simple trick: I followed my finger under the lines. Not to point at each word, but to guide my eyes smoothly across the page. Within ten minutes, my reading speed doubled. I wasn't skipping anything important. I was just grouping words together.  

For example, instead of reading "The cat sat on the mat" as six separate words, I saw it as "The cat sat / on the mat" — two chunks. Then I pushed it further: "The cat sat on the mat" as one single image. That's when my brain clicked. I understood the meaning faster because I stopped fighting with pronunciation. I stopped hearing the voice inside my head.  

Speed reading needs practice. Start with easy texts — news articles, short stories, or even social media posts. Time yourself for one minute. Count how many words you read. Then try to beat that number the next day, but keep your comprehension high. Ask yourself: what did I just read? If you can summarize it in one sentence, you're on the right track.  

There are also tricks like using a pen to underline as you read, or widening your peripheral vision. Some people read three lines at once. Others read only the first and last word of each paragraph. But the real secret is interest. When you love the topic, your brain naturally speeds up. So choose material that excites you.  

This text is around 350 words. Try to finish it in 90 seconds. Then close your eyes and recall three key points. If you remember them well, congratulations — you're not just reading faster, you're thinking faster too. And that is the ultimate goal: not speed for speed's sake, but speed for understanding.
